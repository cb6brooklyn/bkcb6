Liquor Licenses — CB6

Live data from the NYS Liquor Authority. Filtered to CB6. Full Liquor (bars & restaurants) loaded by default — these are the licenses CB6 reviews.

An on-premises liquor license allows a business to sell and serve alcohol for consumption on site, including bars, restaurants, taverns, and hotels. In New York City, applicants must notify the local community board at least 30 days before filing with the State Liquor Authority. That notice requirement also applies to many renewals, alterations, and substantial ownership changes.

Community boards do not approve or deny liquor licenses. We are advisory bodies. What we can do is review applications, hold hearings, work with applicants on stipulations, and submit recommendations or complaints to the State Liquor Authority, which makes the final decision. CB6 reviews full on-premises liquor licenses. Beer and wine license applicants are not reviewed by the board, but the SLA still requires that they provide the community board with 30 days notice before filing.

If the board and an applicant agree on operating conditions, those stipulations can become part of the license and may be enforced by the SLA.

Some applications may also be subject to the 500 Foot Law, which applies when a proposed liquor license is near three existing on-premises liquor licenses. In those cases, the SLA must determine that approving the application is in the public interest. Applicants may now provide the required 30-day notice by email if the community board accepts notices that way — and CB6 does. Email liza@bkcb6.org with your 30-day notice if this applies to you.

The committee meets on the 4th Monday of the month at 6:30 PM and is chaired by Mandi Spishak-Thomas.
