Furman Center — The Bronx Borough Profile

View full profile ↗
Economy

The Bronx has the lowest median income and the highest poverty rate of any borough in New York City. That is not a footnote — it is the defining economic fact of the borough and the starting point for understanding everything else. Belmont and East Tremont (BX6) ranks 59th out of 59 community board districts citywide for median income — dead last. Mott Haven and Melrose (BX1) ranks 56th. Hunts Point and Longwood (BX2) ranks 57th. Highbridge and Concourse (BX4) ranks 54th. Four of the five lowest-income community districts in New York City are in the Bronx.

Riverdale and Fieldston (BX8) is the outlier — it ranks 29th citywide for income, a significant gap from the rest of the borough. Throgs Neck and Co-op City (BX10) ranks 33rd. Morris Park and Bronxdale (BX11) ranks 43rd. But even the highest-income CB in the Bronx sits below the median for the city. The within-borough income contrast is real, but the borough-to-city contrast is more striking. In the Bronx, there is no CB6 equivalent, no district pulling the average up toward the top tier.

Rent & Burden

Rents in the Bronx are lower than in Manhattan, Brooklyn's wealthiest districts, or western Queens — but that lower absolute rent level obscures the burden. Fordham and University Heights (BX5) had a median gross rent of $1,480 in 2023, and 37.7% of renter households were severely rent burdened. That rent burden rate is among the highest in the city. When 38 out of every 100 renter households are spending more than half their income on rent, the affordability crisis is not abstract. The Bronx had the lowest rents in the city — and still couldn't house its residents affordably.

The borough added just 51,502 new housing units between 2010 and 2024 — 15,954 market rate and 35,266 income restricted. That income-restricted share is higher than any other borough's, which reflects both the depth of need and the deliberate targeting of subsidized production here. DOB issued permits for 3,125 new residential units in 2024, 1,735 fewer than in 2023. Certificates of occupancy were issued for 6,552 units, 3,452 fewer than the prior year. Production is slowing, and demand is not.

Housing & Subsidized Stock

8.5% of Bronx rental units were public housing as of 2024 — a higher share than most other boroughs. The borough has 4,286 properties with subsidized units through HUD, LIHTC, 421a, and related programs. 156 of those properties have subsidies expiring between 2025 and 2030. When those subsidies lapse, the units don't automatically convert to market rate — but the protections that keep them affordable do. Tracking expiring subsidies in the Bronx is not a technical exercise. It's a matter of where thousands of families will be able to live.

Demographics

The Bronx had an estimated 1,356,476 residents in 2023. 4.1% identified as Asian, 29.0% as Black, 55.0% as Hispanic, and 8.7% as White. It has the largest proportion of nonwhite population of any borough in the city. The borough's median income is the lowest. Its poverty rate is the highest. Its rent burden is among the worst. These are not unrelated facts.

Source: NYU Furman Center ↗
