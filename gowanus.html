Gowanus Rezoning

The Gowanus neighborhood underwent a major rezoning in 2021 — one of the largest in NYC history — affecting roughly 82 blocks. CB6 was a central and defining force in shaping the final outcome. Over three years, CB6 sent multiple letters to DCP and the Mayor, held a major public hearing, and voted 28–6 to approve with a 14-page resolution of conditions. When the City and City Council reached their final Points of Agreement in November 2021, the overlap with what CB6 had demanded was striking.

CB6 Resolution → Final Points of Agreement

The CB6 resolution and the final Points of Agreement signed into law by Mayor de Blasio and negotiated by Council Members Brad Lander and Stephen Levin overlapped in many ways — on workforce, open space, displaced businesses, and more. A few of the most significant are below.

NYCHA Capital Investment
CB6 made full funding of Gowanus Houses and Wyckoff Gardens a hard condition of its approval. The final POA committed approximately $200 million for comprehensive in-unit renovations at both developments, with resident engagement and anti-displacement protections CB6 had specifically demanded.
Affordability
CB6 demanded deep affordability — 20% of units at an average of 40% of area median income — as the standard across the rezoning area. The final POA went further at Gowanus Green, committing at least 50% of its roughly 950 units at 50% AMI or below.
Combined Sewer Overflow (CSO)
The Gowanus Canal is already one of the most polluted bodies of water in the country. Every time it rains heavily, the city's sewer system overflows and raw sewage flows directly into the Canal — a problem known as combined sewer overflow, or CSO. CB6 demanded that new development not be allowed to make this worse. Specifically, CB6 required the Unified Stormwater Rule — a stricter standard for on-site rainwater management — to be in effect before any new building in the rezoning area could connect to the sewer. The final POA committed to exactly that, ensuring that thousands of new residents moving in do not add to the sewage burden on a Canal that is still being cleaned up by the EPA.
Gowanus Waterfront BID
CB6 demanded a dedicated funding body — a Parks Improvement District — to maintain and program the new waterfront open spaces created by the rezoning, independent of the Parks Department budget. The final POA created the Gowanus Waterfront Business Improvement District for exactly that purpose, with SBS providing technical assistance and a developer-funded steering committee. CB6 called it a Parks Improvement District. The City called it a Waterfront BID. The structure and purpose are the same.
Gowanus Oversight Task Force
CB6's first and hardest condition was an independent community oversight body with real teeth — a funded facilitator, quarterly public meetings, binding agency participation, and a mandate through 2037. The final POA created the Gowanus Community Oversight Task Force on those terms. The GOTF has been meeting since December 2021 and monitors all 56 commitments made by the City during the ULURP process.
