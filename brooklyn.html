Furman Center — Brooklyn Borough Profile

View full profile ↗
Economy

Brooklyn is a borough of stark economic contrast. CB6 — Park Slope, Carroll Gardens, Cobble Hill, Red Hook, and Gowanus — had a median household income of $165,910 in 2023, the 3rd highest among all 59 community board districts citywide. Fort Greene and Brooklyn Heights (CB2) ranks 5th. Greenpoint and Williamsburg (CB1) ranks 11th. These are among the most expensive places to live in the country.

Then there's the other Brooklyn. Brownsville (CB16) had a median income of $43,460 and a poverty rate of 32.4% — the highest in the borough. East New York (CB5) came in at $51,220 with a 22% poverty rate. Coney Island (CB13) ranks 53rd out of 59 citywide for income. Borough Park (CB12) ranks 39th. South Crown Heights and Lefferts Gardens (CB9) ranks 38th. The gap between CB6 and CB16 is not a few thousand dollars. It is a different world, and it exists within the same borough, sometimes within two miles of each other.

Rent & Burden

Median gross rent in CB6 was $2,780 in 2023 — 9th most expensive in the city. Only 14.4% of CB6 renters were severely rent burdened, partly because incomes there are high enough to absorb the cost. That figure doesn't hold for the rest of Brooklyn. In Brownsville, where median rent was $1,160, 32.4% of renters were severely rent burdened — paying more than half their income on rent despite rents that are far lower in absolute terms. East New York had a 29.7% severe rent burden rate on a $1,410 median rent. The problem isn't just rent levels. It's the ratio of rent to income, and across large portions of Brooklyn, that ratio is unsustainable.

Housing Production

Brooklyn added 108,544 new housing units between 2010 and 2024 — 76,513 market rate and 31,996 income restricted. That sounds like a lot. It isn't enough. DOB issued permits for 6,588 new residential units in 2024 and certificates of occupancy for 13,776. The distribution of who those units serve matters as much as the count. In CB6, 81% of new units built since 2010 were market rate. In Brownsville and East New York, the share of income-targeted production was higher — but the total volume was lower, and the need is far greater.

Brooklyn had a rental vacancy rate of just 2.9% as of 2019, the most recent year measured — effectively zero slack in the market. Serious housing code violations ran at 120.8 per 1,000 privately owned rental units in 2023, up from 71.7 in 2000. The stock is aging, demand is not going down, and the gap between what the market builds and what lower-income renters can afford is not closing on its own.

Demographics

Brooklyn had 2,561,225 residents in 2023. 35.9% were foreign-born. 16.2% were 65 or older, up from 11.5% in 2000 — an aging population that puts additional strain on housing and services. 72.4% of commuters used car-free transit as of 2023.
