City & State Budgets

The city and state budget are among the most consequential policy documents produced each year. They determine what gets funded, what gets cut, and what the priorities of government actually are. This tab covers the NYC FY2027 executive budget and the NYS budget as they relate to CB6 and Brooklyn. Content will continue to grow as budget cycles progress.

New York State operates on a fiscal year running April 1 through March 31. The state uses an executive budget model: the Governor develops a comprehensive spending proposal, and the Legislature modifies and enacts it into law. With a budget exceeding $230 billion, the state process directly shapes education funding, transportation capital, housing, and social services that flow into every community district.

DEC
Legislature and Judiciary Submit Budgets
By December 1, the Legislature and Judiciary submit their own funding requests to the Governor. The Governor is constitutionally required to include these figures unchanged in the Executive Budget — the separation of powers prevents the Governor from altering them.
JAN
State of the State & Executive Budget Released
The Governor opens the legislative session with the State of the State address, establishing policy priorities for the year. By mid-January — or February 1 in a post-election year — the Governor submits the Executive Budget to the Legislature along with appropriation bills and Article VII legislation (changes to law required to implement the budget). The Governor has 30 days to amend the proposal after submission.
F–M
Legislative Hearings & One-House Budgets
The Senate Finance Committee and Assembly Ways and Means Committee hold joint public hearings on major program areas — the first formal opportunity for the public and advocacy groups to weigh in. Each chamber then independently develops its own spending proposal, called a one-house budget, reflecting that chamber's priorities. These resolutions do not carry the force of law but define each side's opening position for negotiations. By March 1, the Executive and Legislature must hold a consensus revenue forecasting conference; if they fail to agree, the State Comptroller issues a binding forecast by March 5.
MAR
Three-Way Negotiations
With three separate proposals on the table — the Governor's, the Senate's, and the Assembly's — the Governor, Senate Majority Leader, and Assembly Speaker negotiate a final deal. Most discussions take place privately. The most contested policy items, such as criminal justice provisions, housing mandates, or tax changes, are typically the last to be resolved and most likely to push the process past April 1. Late budgets are common: New York has met its April 1 deadline in only about half of the past 40 years.
APR 1
Budget Enacted · New Fiscal Year Begins
April 1 is the constitutional deadline and the start of the new state fiscal year. Once a deal is reached, both chambers vote on a package of appropriation bills signed into law by the Governor. If no deal is reached by April 1, the Governor and Legislature pass budget extender bills to keep government funded. The Division of the Budget then issues an Enacted Budget Report with official multi-year financial plan projections. NY State's current budget exceeds $230 billion.

New York City operates on a fiscal year running July 1 through June 30. The budget process spans nearly 12 months and involves community boards, borough presidents, the Mayor's Office of Management and Budget (OMB), and the City Council. Community boards are the first formal voice in that process — their district needs statements and budget requests set the foundation before the Mayor's Preliminary Budget is even released.

JUL
New Fiscal Year Begins
July 1 marks the start of the new fiscal year. Community boards immediately begin collecting resident input for the following year's budget — surveys, 311 data, and committee review all inform what gets submitted to the city.
S–O
Community Board District Needs & Budget Requests
CB6 Role
Each of NYC's 59 community boards holds a public hearing, reviews community input, and votes on capital and expense budget priorities. The board submits a formal Statement of District Needs and Budget Requests to the Department of City Planning and OMB by November. CB6's district needs assessment documents the physical and service priorities of our nine neighborhoods — from Red Hook to Park Slope.

JAN
Mayor Releases Preliminary Budget
The Mayor's Office of Management and Budget releases the Preliminary Budget, projecting revenues and expenditures for the coming fiscal year. Agency budget consultations with community boards begin around this time — boards receive the Preliminary Budget Register showing agency responses to their requests.
F–M
CB Preliminary Budget Hearing & Response
CB6 Role
Community boards hold a public hearing on the Preliminary Budget and submit a letter of comment to OMB and the Department of City Planning. This is a second formal opportunity to press agencies on unmet budget requests and flag gaps in service delivery.
APR
Mayor Releases Executive Budget
The Executive Budget reflects revised revenue estimates and updated spending priorities. Borough Boards — composed of Borough Presidents, Council Members, and CB chairs — submit their formal responses. The City Council begins hearings on the Executive Budget.
MAY
City Council Hearings & Negotiations
The Council holds public hearings on the Executive Budget by agency. Negotiations between the Council and the Mayor determine final funding levels. Council Members advocate for district priorities — community board requests that made it into agency responses carry the most weight here.
JUN 30
Budget Adopted
The City Council votes to adopt the budget by June 30 — the constitutional deadline. The adopted budget takes effect July 1, starting the cycle again. NYC's FY2026 adopted budget totaled $115.9 billion.
