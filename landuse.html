CB6 Land Use & Zoning

The Zoning Resolution of 1916 — the first citywide zoning code in the United States — and the changes it has seen over the last century, form the basis for most of what you see walking every block of New York City and, of course, Brooklyn Community Board 6's district.

For CB6, this is not abstract. Community board actions, public hearings, ULURP applications, committee work, and everyday civic debates often concern how these policy choices play out in real life. This guide is here to help with that. If you spend a little time with it, R, M, and C start to mean something deeper than a single letter on a map.

You do not need to be a planner to have a real stake in zoning — or to bring the kind of lived perspective that leads to better decisions. The goal here is simple: help people understand the language of zoning well enough to better determine who gets what, when, and how.

🏗 ULURP — Uniform Land Use Review Procedure
The standard public review for major land use actions — rezonings, special permits, city map changes. Strict timelines apply.
1. Certified by DCP — Starts the clock. All deadlines run from here.
2. CB6 (60 days) — Public hearing + vote. Advisory recommendation forwarded to Borough President.
3. Borough President (30 days) — Issues a recommendation.
4. CPC (60 days) — Public hearing. Approves, modifies, or disapproves.
5. City Council (50 days) — May approve, modify, or disapprove CPC decision.
6. Mayor (5 days) — May veto; Council may override.

Handles variances and special permits outside ULURP — unique hardships or zoning flexibility for specific uses.
1. Application Filed — No fixed start date. No strict timeline clock.
2. CB6 (45 days) — Written recommendation submitted. Advisory only.
3. BSA Public Hearing — Applicant presents. Public may testify. Multiple hearings common.
4. BSA Vote — Approve, approve with conditions, or deny. No City Council review.

SEQR (Article 8, Environmental Conservation Law) requires state and local agencies to assess the environmental impact of any discretionary action before approving it — including rezonings, special permits, and public projects. For CB6, this most commonly comes up alongside ULURP applications. The lead agency conducts the review; CB6's role is to participate and comment.
1. Action Classification — Is the action Type I (more likely significant), Unlisted (case-by-case), or Type II (exempt)? Type II actions need no further review.
2. Lead Agency Designated — When multiple agencies are involved, one is designated lead. The lead coordinates the full environmental review.
3. Environmental Assessment Form (EAF) — Applicant completes Part 1. Lead agency completes Parts 2 & 3 to assess significance.
4. SEQR Determination — Lead agency issues one of three findings: Negative Declaration (no significant impact), Conditioned Negative Declaration (impacts manageable with conditions), or Positive Declaration (significant impact — EIS required).
5. Environmental Impact Statement (EIS) — Required only on a Positive Declaration. Draft EIS is scoped, prepared, released for public comment, and a Final EIS issued before any agency decision.
6. Findings Statement — After the Final EIS, the lead agency issues a Findings Statement weighing environmental impact against social and economic factors before making a decision.

🏘 Residential (R) Districts
R2 — Low-density detached housing. Single-family homes, large lots. Found in parts of Red Hook.
R4 / R4A / R4B — Low-to-medium density. Row houses, semi-detached homes, small apartment buildings. Max ~1.35 FAR. Common in Carroll Gardens and Red Hook.
R6 / R6A / R6B — Medium density. The most common residential zone in CB6. R6A and R6B are contextual — they require buildings to match the scale of the surrounding block. Max FAR 2.43–3.0.
R7A / R7B / R7X — Medium-high density. Larger apartment buildings. Found along 4th Avenue and parts of the Gowanus rezoning area. Max FAR 3.44–5.0.
R8 / R8A — High density. Tall residential buildings. Limited in CB6 but present near major corridors. Max FAR up to 7.52.
🏪 Commercial (C) Districts
C1 / C2 — Neighborhood retail overlays. Usually mapped over residential zones to allow ground-floor retail (grocery, laundry, restaurants). The R zone controls residential uses above.
C4 — General commercial. Larger stores, offices, entertainment. Allows more intense uses than C1/C2. Found along major corridors in CB6.
C6 — High-density commercial. Major office and retail corridors. Allows the tallest commercial buildings.
🏭 Manufacturing (M) Districts
M1 — Light manufacturing. Allows a wide range of industrial uses plus some commercial. Often buffers between heavier industrial and residential. Concentrated in Gowanus and Red Hook.
M2 — Medium manufacturing. Heavier industrial uses than M1. Requires greater separation from residential. Present in parts of Red Hook.
M3 — Heavy manufacturing. The most intensive industrial uses — waste treatment, power plants, etc. Not common in CB6.
📐 Key Terms
FAR (Floor Area Ratio) — Total floor area of a building divided by the lot area. FAR 2.0 on a 5,000 sq ft lot = max 10,000 sq ft of building.
Contextual Zoning (A/B suffixes) — R6A, R7A, etc. are contextual zones that require buildings to match the existing neighborhood scale through street wall and height limits.
Use Group — NYC groups land uses into 18 categories (UG1–UG18). Each zone permits certain use groups. Retail is UG6, community facilities are UG3/UG4, heavy industry is UG17/UG18.
Overlay District — A commercial zone (e.g. C1-3) mapped on top of a residential zone, adding retail permissions to the ground floor only.
Special District — A custom zoning framework layered on top of base zoning for areas with unique characteristics. The Gowanus Special Mixed Use District (Chapter 9) is an example.
