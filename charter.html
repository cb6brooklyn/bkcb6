🏛️ The NYC City Charter

What it is, why it matters, and CB6's role in shaping it

What Is the City Charter?

The City Charter is New York City's governing document — consider it the city's constitution. It defines the structure of city government, the powers and responsibilities of elected officials, and the rules that govern how the city makes decisions that affect all of us.

A Charter Revision Commission (CRC) reviews the charter and proposes updates that ultimately appear as ballot questions for voters to decide. These changes have shaped the very structure of our city at critical moments — and continue to do so today.

📅 A History of Charter Change

1898
The Five Boroughs. A charter revision led to the consolidation of the five boroughs into New York City — a move approved by Brooklyn voters by a mere 267 votes. Some, including myself at times, have jokingly referred to this as the "mistake of '98." The point is this: charter changes have lasting, wide-reaching impact.
1975
Community Boards & ULURP. A CRC led to the creation of Community Boards and the Uniform Land Use Review Procedure (ULURP) — the public review process that CB6 uses today to weigh in on land use applications in our neighborhoods.
2018
CB Term Limits. A CRC brought about term limits for community board members, adding accountability and turnover to local civic participation.
2024
Expanded Mayoral Powers. Voters saw five CRC-generated ballot proposals — most of which expanded mayoral power — four of which passed. Yet most people hardly remember voting on them. That disconnect is exactly why public engagement matters.
2025
The Commission to Strengthen Local Democracy. The City Council formed a new CRC. Brooklyn Borough President Antonio Reynoso appointed CB6 District Manager Mike Racioppo as a Commissioner to represent Brooklyn's communities in this process.

Why It Matters for CB6

The charter directly shapes what community boards can and cannot do. Two reforms I've focused on as a Commissioner:

Comprehensive Planning. We need a citywide planning process to replace the current piecemeal, project-by-project approach — one that incorporates macro-scale needs instead of isolated interests. Right now, decisions about housing, infrastructure, and land use happen in silos. That has to change.

An Independent Community Board Office. Borough President Reynoso has proposed an independent CB Office that would give community boards the professional staffing and support they need to effectively represent their neighborhoods. In CB6 alone, we've lost approximately 1,500 housing units since 2010 due to consolidation in landmark districts — including 388 units since the start of COVID, the most of any community district citywide. CBs need the capacity to track and respond to trends like these.

CB6 & Landmarks Reform

Our current preservation framework can unintentionally exacerbate the housing crisis — particularly through the unchecked consolidation of housing units in landmarked districts. Reforming the Landmarks Preservation Commission (LPC) to acknowledge and address these outcomes is critical if we want preservation policies that complement, rather than undermine, our housing and equity goals.

CB6 Land Use Coordinator Rebecca Kobert has been researching this trend in depth, and the data is clear: the neighborhoods we preserve must also be neighborhoods people can afford to live in.

Your Voice in the Process

Whether you agree or disagree with any of these ideas, your voice belongs in this conversation. The charter has shaped New York City for over a century. Charter changes have lasting, wide-reaching impact — and they happen with or without public input. The question is whether New Yorkers show up to shape them.

If you have thoughts, questions, or ideas about charter reform and what it means for CB6, reach out to the board at Mike@bkcb6.org.
